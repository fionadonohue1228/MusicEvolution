Echoes Through Time

How Music Has Evolved Across Genres and Decades

Research Question

How has music evolved across different genres and time periods, and what patterns emerge when we examine these changes holistically?

Introduction

Music is one of humanity's most universal forms of expression, yet it constantly evolving, shifting, merging, and reinventing itself across cultures and time periods. Studying this evolution offers insight into the broader cultural, technological, and social changes that shape human experience. Our project examines how the music industry has changed over time by using Spotify Track Attributes and Popularity data to compare what made songs popular in different eras.

This approach is compelling because listeners often don't think about why they like or dislike a song. By analyzing attributes such as tempo, energy, danceability, and instrumentation, we can identify what contributes to a song's success. This allows us to model and predict not only what makes music popular today, but also what drove popularity in the past.

Musical change never happens in isolation. Genres emerge from specific historical moments and respond to new technologies and cultural movements. These forces shape the measurable features of popular music, revealing patterns in how styles develop, spread, and transform. Examining these patterns holistically helps us understand how music evolves and what distinguishes today's sound from that of previous decades.

By blending cultural context with data-driven analysis, we gain a deeper understanding of music's continuous evolution and the factors that shape what listeners respond to across time.

Introduction to the Dataset

Our analysis uses the Spotify Tracks Attributes and Popularity dataset from Kaggle, which originates directly from Spotify's API. This comprehensive dataset provides a robust foundation for examining music evolution across genres and time periods.

The complete dataset contains approximately 114,000 rows with 20 columns of attributes. For our analysis, we used the first 15,000 rows (individual tracks), which provides a substantial and manageable sample size for identifying patterns and trends across different musical eras and genres while maintaining computational efficiency.

Key Attributes

The dataset includes several categories of attributes that enable multifaceted analysis:

Identifying Information:

  • Track ID - Unique identifier for each song
  • Artists - Artist or artists who performed the track
  • Track name - Song title for identification
  • Album name - The album containing the track
  • Track genre - Musical genre classification

Audio Features (Numeric):

  • Danceability - Measures how suitable a track is for dancing (0.0 to 1.0)
  • Energy - Represents intensity and activity level (0.0 to 1.0)
  • Acousticness - Confidence measure of whether the track is acoustic (0.0 to 1.0)
  • Valence - Captures the musical positiveness/mood conveyed by a track (0.0 to 1.0)
  • Tempo - Beats per minute (BPM)
  • Loudness - Overall volume in decibels (dB)
  • Speechiness - Detects presence of spoken words (0.0 to 1.0)
  • Instrumentalness - Predicts whether a track contains vocals (0.0 to 1.0)
  • Liveness - Detects presence of an audience in the recording (0.0 to 1.0)
  • Duration_ms - Track length in milliseconds
  • Key - The key the track is in
  • Mode - Major or minor modality
  • Time signature - Estimated overall time signature

Popularity Metrics:

  • Popularity - Spotify's algorithm-based popularity score (0-100)

Temporal Information:

  • Year/Release date - When the track was released (pulled from Spotify API as supplementary data)

Data Types and Genre Coverage

The dataset contains 16 numeric attributes and 4 character (categorical) attributes. Since we're working with the first 15,000 rows rather than the full dataset, our genre representation is more limited than what would be available in the complete 114,000-row dataset. This means our analysis focuses on the genres that appear most frequently in this subset. While this narrows our scope, it still allows for meaningful analysis of major genre trends and evolution patterns.

Why This Data Works

This dataset subset is particularly valuable because it combines objective, algorithmically-measured audio features with metadata that provides cultural context. The audio features allow us to quantify what makes different genres distinct and how they've changed, while the artist, album, genre, and temporal information lets us understand the cultural and historical dimensions of these changes. Even with 15,000 tracks, the sample size is sufficient to identify statistically significant patterns within the genres represented, making it suitable for studying music evolution holistically.

Visualizations

Now that we understand the data behind our analysis, let's explore what it reveals. Our journey begins with the most striking finding: how dramatically successful music has transformed over the past four decades.

Visualization 1: Hit Songs Evolution (1980-2020)

Key Takeaways:

What does success sound like? The answer has changed dramatically. This vizualization reveals how the features of successful music (songs with a 70.0+ popularity score) changed over four decades. The most striking trend is the dramatic decline of valence, dropping from 82% in 1980 to just 51% by 2020, demonstrating music's shift away from happiness, with hit songs becoming noticeably darker. Meanwhile, danceability showed an increase from 45% in 1990 to 69% in 2020, reflecting the growing influence of club culture and electronic dance music. Energy levels remained relatively stable with minor fluctuations, suggesting that intensity has always been a component of successful music regardless of era. These patterns indicate that modern hit songs are characterized by electronic production, darker content, and danceable rhythms, a stark contrast to the high valence hits of the 1980s.

Visualization 2: Genre Audio Profiles

The transformation raises a compelling question: Why did successful music change so dramatically?

To understand the answer, we need to meet the players in this evolution, which are the genres themselves, each with their own unique sonic DNA...

Key Takeaways:

This comparative analysis reveals audio characteristics that make each music genre unique, and when we connect these genre profiles to the temporal trends observed in Visualization 1, predictive patterns emerge. Genres heavily dependent on acoustic sound, such as acoustic, bluegrass, and ambient music, face potential challenges in mainstream popularity, as our data shows acoustic characteristics declining by 17% in hit songs from 1985 to 2020. The same goes for songs that rely heavily on a high valence like childrens music, might face challenges becoming hit songs. Conversely, genres like chicago-house and club music, which exhibit high danceability and electronic production, align closely with the characteristics that define modern successful music. Genre success depends on alignment with evolving listener preferences. Genres with trending characteristics (electronic, danceable) are positioned for broader success, while acoustic-focused genres may face reduced mainstream appeal despite maintaining dedicated audiences.

Understanding the players is crucial, but there's another dimension to this story: the stakes have changed.

We've seen how success transformed, met the players with their unique identities, and witnessed how the stakes became unpredictable. But there's more to discover: how has the entire musical ecosystem evolved over time?

Let's explore the broader patterns of change across the decades...

Visualization 4: Music Evolution

Key Takeaways:

Over time, popular music has transformed from a relatively narrow field into a richly diversified landscape spanning nearly every combination of energy and danceability. While the 1960s featured only two genres, acoustic and afrobeats, clustered in mid-range zones, the 2010s to 2020s show dense, wide-ranging distribution as new genres spawned. Energy levels have steadily risen, with club music favoring high-energy sounds, and danceability has similarly migrated upward, influenced by club and afrobeat production. Despite this expansion, core genre identities remain consistent—ambient stays low-energy, afrobeat and club dominate high-energy/high-danceability areas, and acoustic's music holds steady in moderate-energy, high-danceability territory. Popular tracks increasingly cluster in the “sweet spot” of moderately high energy and high danceability, even as niche genres continue to thrive outside mainstream zones. Overall, the musical ecosystem has become far more varied, democratized, and rhythm-driven.

As the musical landscape expanded and diversified, another fundamental change was happening beneath the surface: how long music lasts.

Song duration tells its own story about attention spans, commercial pressures, and changing listening habits...

Visualization 5: Song Duration Over the Decades

Key Takeaways:

This visualization is a D3 heatmap that shows the duration of music across different decades. It reveals that from the 1950s through the 1970s, most songs were relatively short, generally staying below around 300–400 seconds. There are very few longer tracks during this period. Songs from the 2010s to 2020s show much more variation in duration. While most modern songs still stay under 300 seconds, there are a few noticeable outliers with longer songs. In the 1950s, the majority of songs were below 200 seconds; however, in the 2020s, the music length is more spread, with most music being between 150 seconds and 300 seconds. Also, over the decades, there is a change in the amount of genres. The 1950s had very little variety in music but as the decades progressed there was a significant change in the amount of genres. This feature also demonstrates how long different genres songs are, children songs are typically shorter, while chicago-house is typically longer. Overall, this suggests that earlier decades tended to have consistently shorter songs, while later decades include a wider mix of both shorter and longer tracks. This also shows an influx in music in the 2000s compared to the mid-1900s.

Conclusion

Our visualizations reveal a few things about the data. Visualization 1 shows that while features such as speechiness, energy, and acousticness exhibit less variability, there is a significant downward trend in the feature valence over the years, suggesting that music has become less positive in recent years. In Visualization 2, across the decades from 1950 to the 2020s, ambient music has the highest level of acousticness but also the lowest danceability, energy, and valence. In Visualization 3, songs from the 2010s to 2020s shows greater variability in popularity compared to songs from earlier decades. Songs in the 2010s and 2020s are more popular than songs in earlier decades, likely due to the internet and the growth of social media throughout the years.

It is a consistent finding that songs in earlier decades are shorter in length, as shown in Visualization 5. After the 2000s, there is more spread in song length, with a large portion falling between 150 and 300 seconds. This also demonstrates the increase in genres over the years and varying durations. Typically, children's music is shorter while Chicago house music is longer in seconds. In Visualization 4, the increase in genres between 1950 and 2020 is shown. This provides a greater spread in genres. It illustrates club music having more energy and danceability, while ambient music has less energy and danceability.

Overall, our data has changed over the decades. This is because of mood, popularity variability, and style. Some features have stayed the same throughout the decades. Some future work could include comparing Spotify’s trends to other music platforms like Apple Music or Amazon Music. With this, we would be able to see whether these trends stay consistent across platforms.
